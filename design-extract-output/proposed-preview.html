At-Tamassok

Thalia Bassim · Brooklyn, 2024

The kitchen had been someone else's before it was mine, and before that someone else's again. I knew this because the tile behind the stove carried three different layers of grout, each a slightly different shade of white, each applied by a hand I would never meet. The photographs in this series began there, with the grout.

What I was looking for, though I did not know it yet, was the way light behaved in rooms that had been lived in long enough to forget who had lived in them. A glass of tea left on a windowsill. Steam catching gold from an angle that would not exist in twenty minutes. The particular quality of attention that a room demands when you are alone in it and the afternoon is long.

In Beirut the kitchens were different but the rituals were the same. A stovetop flame adjusted without looking. Bread torn, not cut. The sound of water in a building where the plumbing remembers a different era. I photographed these things not because they were beautiful — though some of them were — but because they repeated. The repetition was the point. Domestic gestures that cross continents and never quite translate, that resist the passage of years by refusing to change.

At-Tamassok, in Arabic, means something close to “holding on” or “clinging to.” Not in desperation but in dailiness. The way your hand finds the same spot on the countertop each morning. The way a cup is placed, not put down. Twenty-five frames over three years, all shot on Portra 400 because its rendering of gold is the closest I have found to the color of that kitchen light at four in the afternoon.
